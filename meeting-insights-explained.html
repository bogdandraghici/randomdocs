How a messy session recording becomes a clean, reviewable set of insights — and what happens to them next.

For  The product team Subject  The Meeting Insights skill Focus  How insights are collected & processed

In one sentence: Meeting Insights takes a raw user-testing or meeting transcript and turns it into a clean, browsable page of insight “cards” your team can review, edit, and act on.

What you give it

  • 1A transcript of the session — or just the recording, which it transcribes for you first.
  • 2The video file, if you have it — used to read the screen and settle unclear words by listening.
  • 3A sentence or two of context: what was tested or discussed, who was there, and the goal.

What you get back

  • →One self-contained web page — nothing to install, opens in any browser.
  • →The cleaned-up transcript plus filterable insight cards you can edit in place.
  • →A built-in review loop to send your changes back, and an English / Romanian toggle.

01
Intake

Setting the scene

Before touching anything, the tool collects three things: the transcript (or a recording to transcribe), the recording file if it exists, and a short brief on the session.

That brief does more work than it looks. It tells the tool which odd-looking words are actually product terms, who each person is, and which findings matter. It also settles the session type — and that single choice changes how every insight gets credited.

User-testing · one subject

A single person is observed

Insights capture the tester's reactions and stance — not the facilitator's questions. When an interviewer floats a hypothesis, the insight is what the participant said back, credited to them.

Meeting · peers

Equals discussing together

No single subject. Each insight is credited to whoever actually voiced it, and the tool tracks where a discussion landed — recording the conclusion, not every idea floated and dropped along the way.

02
Repair

Cleaning up the words

These sessions are spoken in Romanian with English product terms sprinkled in — and the automatic transcriber mangles the technical words phonetically (oligbeis for “Knowledge Base”, MZP for “MCP”). Repair restores the real words while keeping each sentence natural.

The important part: it never quietly guesses. Every fix is logged, sorted into one of three honesty buckets so nothing is hidden.

auto
Confident fixes
oligbeis · nowligebase → Knowledge Base

Obvious typos and known product terms. Applied silently.

confirm
Best guesses to verify
“Jessica” → reconstructed from context (reasoning noted)

Plausible reconstructions flagged for a human to confirm.

query
Genuinely unclear
unrecoverable token → logged as a question to ask you

Meaningful but unreadable — never papered over.

Why this matters Insights are only as good as the transcript. Extracting ideas from garbled text produces garbled ideas, so the whole point of this stage is to produce a clean base before any analysis begins. Uncertain spots stay visibly marked right in the transcript.
03
Who said what

Getting the speakers right

Automatic transcription is unreliable about who spoke — it invents extra speakers, and it staples lines to the wrong person (worst of all on quick “mhm / yes / correct” interjections). Because every insight is tied to who said it, a wrong speaker quietly poisons the analysis.

So the tool re-checks attribution before extracting, using:

Role & expertise

The PM states product facts; the designer narrates the screen they built; the engineer explains internals. A line's content betrays its owner.

Self-reference & names

“my prototype”, “I made this” ties a line to its owner. Nobody names themselves in the third person — “I called Bogdan” means the speaker isn't Bogdan.

Voice fingerprinting

When the recording allows it, actual voice analysis separates people who sound alike — the one thing content clues can't do reliably (e.g. two men).

Listening to the audio

For anything still in doubt, the moment is played back to hear who actually spoke — preferred over any guess.

The rule Anything it can't settle confidently is flagged, never silently assigned — and any insight leaning on that line is marked as needing confirmation, so it reaches you in the review.
04
Extract · screen context

Turning talk into insights

Only now — on a clean, correctly-attributed transcript — does the tool pull out insights, one per card. Each card is anchored back to the exact transcript lines it came from, so any claim can be traced to its source in one click.

When a video exists, there's an extra touch: sessions constantly point at things on screen (“this icon”, “that table”). The tool grabs a still frame at the right moment and pins it to the relevant card — after checking it didn't catch a blank or loading screen — so a screen-dependent finding explains itself.

05
Clarify & review

Confirming and refining, together

The tool surfaces what it doesn't know instead of guessing past it, and gives you a way to fix it. Three mechanisms handle that:

  • ✳Nothing is guessed silently. Every uncertain word or speaker is flagged and visible.
  • ✳Open questions come one at a time. After the first draft, it walks you through each unclear item as a single, self-contained question — never a wall of questions to answer at once. You can answer or skip each one.
  • ✳Your answers fold back in. Once settled, the report rebuilds with the fixes applied and the “open questions” count drops.

The review loop — the page reviews you back

The report page is interactive. You can edit any card, comment on anything, or uncheck cards you don't want to keep. One button bundles just your changes into a packet; hand it back and the tool folds them in and rebuilds the report.

06
Pool & publish

What happens to insights next

An insight is most useful when it can travel beyond its own session. Three things make that possible:

🧷

Every insight is self-describing

Each one can be exported stamped with its session, feature, date, and who said it — so it stays meaningful when pooled with insights from other sessions in a shared database, no matter where it ends up.

🌐

Reports publish to your team's sessions repo

With one command a report goes to the sessions repo you've set up for your instance of the skill, organised by feature area — newest first — as a browsable site so your team can find and compare sessions in one place.

📈

A shared glossary accumulates across sessions

Each run adds newly-confirmed product terms to a shared glossary. That glossary feeds the transcriber, so later sessions' transcripts arrive with fewer mangled terms to repair.

Language toggle The whole report has an English / Romanian toggle. It flips the analysis — headlines, details, topics — into Romanian, while the original quotes stay verbatim as spoken. Everyone reads it in the language they prefer without losing the evidence.

Setup

Installing it

Installed like any other FlowX skill: find it under /plugins in the FlowX marketplace and install from there. Once it's installed, just ask Claude to run it — Claude handles most of the first-time setup itself.

Claude sets this up

Ask Claude to run it — it handles this

  • ffmpeg — installed with brew install ffmpeg if it's missing. Only needed to transcribe a raw recording or pull screen frames.
  • The transcription library — installs itself into a local .venv the first time it runs.
You do this yourself

A few things Claude can't do for you

  • Get a Gemini API key and export it as GEMINI_API_KEY — it's tied to your own account, so Claude can't generate it for you.
  • Run gh auth login once — only needed to publish reports to your team's sessions repo. It's an interactive login only you can complete.
  • For voice fingerprinting (the acoustic speaker pass): get a free Hugging Face token, accept the gated model terms on huggingface.co for both pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1 and pyannote/segmentation-3.0, and export the token as HF_TOKEN. Only needed if that pass is used — plain transcription doesn't need it.
